What happens when all the words die?

An argument that the institutions we rely on to distinguish new human knowledge from synthetic noise are being outpaced by the rate at which that noise is now produced.

Most of my research centers on supply chain fraud, and in particular on interorganizational deception — cases in which one party to a commercial relationship misrepresents what it is actually providing. Horsemeat sold as beef in Europe in 2013. Falsified aluminum certifications used by NASA, which the supplier admitted to in 2019 and which contributed to two failed satellite launches. Counterfeit plastic in the accelerator arms of roughly 17,000 Aston Martins between 2007 and 2012. Lead-based paint applied by a Mattel subsupplier that circumvented a testing laboratory the buyer had paid to install. These cases share a structural feature. They occur at points in an exchange where verifying what the other party actually delivered has become sufficiently expensive that routine verification is no longer economically rational.

This essay argues that a functionally similar condition has emerged in the production and circulation of written knowledge. Generative AI has reduced the cost of producing plausible, grammatically fluent prose by several orders of magnitude over a few years. The downstream consequence is a verification problem at institutional scale that the systems we rely on — peer review, editorial vetting, grading, citation — were not designed to handle at the current prevalence of synthetic content. I document the evidence across four domains: student coursework, peer-reviewed academic publishing, the open web, and the data used to train successor models. In each, the measured rate of synthetic content has grown faster than the capacity to reliably distinguish it from human writing.

My reading of the evidence is that this is a present-tense condition rather than a hypothetical future one, and that its implications for knowledge institutions have not yet been widely internalized.

The cost of a word

The first consideration is economic. The marginal cost of producing a grammatical, readable paragraph has fallen by several orders of magnitude over the span of four years. Gartner forecast in 2021 that 60% of the data used to develop AI and analytics projects would be synthetically generated by 2024, up from roughly 1% in 2021C1. One industry estimate places the synthetic data generation market at approximately $310M in 2024, with reported growth of about 35% per yearC4. Output-token pricing from the major commercial labs fell roughly 75-fold between 2021 and 2025, moving the effective cost of producing written text from pennies per thousand words toward pennies per million.

A price change of this magnitude affects the economics of attribution. The infrastructure used to certify who wrote what — bylines, citations, editorial review, peer review, institutional reputation — developed under the implicit assumption that producing written content in quantity was sufficiently costly that it generally only made sense when the author had something to contribute. That assumption is weaker now than it was. The cost of producing plausible text has become trivial, while the cost of verifying that a specific piece of text reflects the reasoning of a specific human has not fallen in parallel.

The two costs have diverged. Production costs dropped rapidly; detection costs did not, and by several accounts detector accuracy against newer model generations has declined. The asymmetry has practical consequences. A party producing synthetic content faces fewer barriers each year, while a party attempting to verify authorship faces more.

The dynamic here is familiar from a much older economic setting. Gresham's law, originally formulated about coinage, describes what happens when lower-quality instruments circulate at the same nominal value as higher-quality ones: the better instruments are withdrawn from circulation, and the worse ones define the market. An analogous pattern applies to written content once the cost of producing low-signal text falls below the cost of distinguishing it from the rest.

Academia has already fallen

The domain in which the transition is most precisely measurable is the one in which I have the most direct professional exposure: peer-reviewed scientific publishing. Several recent studies document the rate at which large language model output has entered the published literature, and the findings are consistent across methodologies and disciplines.

A team led by Weixin Liang at Stanford analyzed more than 1.1 million papers and preprints across arXiv, bioRxiv, and the Nature portfolio from January 2020 through September 2024, using a sentence-level classifier to estimate the share of text that had been LLM-modified. By February 2024, roughly 17.5% of computer-science paper abstracts showed evidence of LLM modification. By September 2024, the share approached 22%B1. The same methodology returned 4.9% for mathematics abstracts and 6.3% for the Nature portfolio — lower absolute values, but on similar upward trajectories.

A separate study by Kobak and colleagues of more than 15 million PubMed biomedical abstracts found LLM signatures in at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts, with the share reaching 40% in some subcorporaB2. The detection method relied on tracking word-frequency anomalies. Certain words rose sharply in frequency across 2023 and 2024 in patterns consistent with LLM-generated prose. Uses of the word “delve” in biomedical abstracts, for example, rose from 349 in 2020 to 2,847 in 2023B3, an approximately eightfold increase in three years, with similar increases documented for “intricate,” “meticulous,” and “commendable.”

Journals and publishers have begun to respond. Springer's Neurosurgical Review retracted 129 papers in early 2025 for undisclosed LLM-generated text, of which 87 traced to a single universityB4. Retraction Watch maintains a running tally of papers and peer reviews flagged for characteristic LLM phrases such as “certainly, here is” and “as of my last knowledge update” left in the submitted text; the tally stands at 92 papers and two peer reviews as of this writingB5. A follow-up study by Liang and colleagues applied the same sentence-level classifier to the peer-review side and estimated that 10.6% of reviewer text at ICLR 2024 and 16.9% at EMNLP had been substantially modified by ChatGPTB6.

Taken together, these findings describe a system in which the same tool is being used at scale both to produce the submitted literature and to evaluate it. The retraction mechanism is operating, but it is reacting after publication and at a pace substantially slower than the rate at which LLM-modified papers are entering the literature. Retraction is, in other words, a lagging indicator, and the studies above suggest the lag is currently measured in years.

The student layer

Student adoption of generative AI for coursework has moved faster than any comparable technology transition documented in recent surveys of higher education. In several major samples, the transition is effectively complete at the undergraduate level.

The Higher Education Policy Institute's 2025 survey asked UK undergraduates whether they had used generative AI for their studies. In 2024, 66% said yes. In 2025, 92% said yes. The share using AI directly on assessed work rose from 53% to 88% over the same yearA1. The cohort reporting no use fell from roughly one-third to roughly one-twelfth in twelve months. In survey-research terms, a single-year shift of this magnitude is the kind of change typically associated with regulatory mandates or platform-native features becoming default behaviors, rather than with voluntary adoption of a discretionary tool.

The international picture is the same. The Digital Education Council's 2024 survey of students across 16 countries found 86% using AI in their studies, with 54% using it weeklyA2. Tyton Partners reported 59% of U.S. college students using generative AI at least monthly for schoolwork, up from 43% the prior springA6. OpenAI's own data put more than a third of U.S. 18–24-year-olds on regular ChatGPT use, with more than a quarter of their messages classified as schoolworkA7.

The pattern extends downward to secondary school. 26% of U.S. teens reported having used ChatGPT for schoolwork in 2024, double the 13% share measured the year beforeA3. By late 2025, 59% of U.S. teens were using ChatGPT and roughly 30% were using AI chatbots dailyA4. Common Sense Media's 2024 survey found 40% of teens using generative AI for school assignments, and 46% of those did so without teacher permissionA5.

The pedagogical consequence depends on a straightforward claim about skill acquisition: writing is a skill that develops through sustained practice, under conditions in which the practitioner is generating the output themselves. Where the majority of a student's written work during a degree program has been produced in collaboration with a language model, the student has practiced a substantially different skill from the one the degree was designed to teach and certify. The question that follows is therefore an empirical one about what graduates of the current cohort can actually do without assistance, rather than a question about the moral character of individual students.

Framing the situation primarily as an academic integrity problem — that is, as a detection-and-enforcement problem — understates the scope. Once a practice shifts from a fringe behavior to a majority behavior within a single academic cycle, detection-based responses are working against the base rate rather than against the exception, and the evidentiary and procedural costs of enforcement rise correspondingly.

The synthetic flood

The pattern observed in academic publishing is less advanced than the one observed on the open web. In November 2024, according to Graphite's analysis of approximately 65,000 URLs sampled from CommonCrawl, the share of new English-language web articles predominantly written by AI passed the share predominantly written by humans for the first timeD2. By May 2025, Graphite reported that share at 52%. A separate analysis by Ahrefs of 900,000 newly detected web pages in April 2025 found that 74.2% contained some AI-generated content, with 25.8% classified as entirely human-writtenD1.

The pattern extends beyond text. Deezer's internal detection tool reported in September 2025 that 28% of new music uploads to the platform were fully AI-generated. By April 2026 the share had reached 44%, which Deezer estimates at roughly 75,000 AI-generated tracks per day, up from approximately 10,000 per day at the start of 2025D4. Originality.ai's longitudinal study of Google's top-20 search results reported AI-content share at 17.31% in September 2025, compared with a February 2019 baseline of 2.27%D6. Pangram Labs reported that 3% of front-page reviews on Amazon best-sellers were AI-generated; 93% of those reviews carried a Verified Purchase badge, and 74% were five starsD5. NewsGuard, which tracks unreliable AI-generated news and information sites, counted 3,006 such sites across 16 languages as of March 2026D3. Graphika, analyzing a different segment of the same broader phenomenon, identified more than 10,000 AI character-chatbots with labels indicating sexualized minor personas across five platformsD7.

These studies were conducted independently by different research teams, using different detection methodologies, measured against different corpora, and with different denominators. What they share is that each one documents substantial upward movement in the AI share of newly produced content in the channel it measured. The convergence of findings across methods and media does not prove causation in any one case, but the consistency of the direction is difficult to attribute to methodological artifact alone.

The practical implication for downstream consumers of web content — researchers, educators, journalists, institutional buyers, the reading public — is that the baseline assumption of human authorship for newly published online content can no longer be sustained without explicit verification, and in most consumer-facing contexts that verification is not currently available.

The Ouroboros

A related concern involves the composition of the training data used for the next generation of language models. Large models are typically trained on crawls of the open web. As documented in the prior section, the open web is now substantially composed of AI-generated content. Successive model generations are therefore increasingly being trained on the output of prior model generations, with the proportion of human-authored material in each successive training corpus declining over time.

A team led by Ilia Shumailov published a paper in Nature in 2024 that investigated the consequences of this pattern experimentally. When successive generations of a language model were trained on data produced by their predecessors, the distribution of learnable concepts narrowed in a measurable way from generation to generation. Rare tokens, marginal ideas, and outlier perspectives disappeared from the model's learned distribution first. Within roughly nine recursive generations in the experimental setup, perplexity diverged substantially from the original distribution. The authors termed this pattern “model collapse”C2.

There is an important distinction to preserve here. Gartner's 2021 forecast that 60% of training data would be synthetic by 2024 referred to deliberate, curated synthetic data generated by model developers to fill specific gaps in their training setsC1. That is a different phenomenon from the one documented by the Ahrefs and Graphite studies, in which 74.2% of newly indexed web pages contain machine-generated output that was not produced or audited by any model developer, and that is included in the next crawl by defaultD1. The concern is specifically about the ambient, uncurated case rather than the curated one.

The Epoch AI research group has published an analysis projecting that the effective stock of publicly available human-generated text, which they estimate at roughly 300 trillion tokens, will be exhausted at current training-data consumption rates sometime between 2026 and 2032, with a median estimate near 2028C3. The date at which ambient synthetic content becomes a material input to frontier model training is therefore within the normal planning horizon for training-data decisions. The operative uncertainty concerns the rate at which different labs adopt filtering approaches to exclude AI-generated content from their training corpora, and the extent to which such filtering remains feasible as detector performance against newer models declines.

A training regime in which the model distribution is increasingly shaped by prior model output reduces the corrective pressure previously provided by anchoring on human-generated text. Each successive generation inherits a narrower slice of the original distribution than the one before it, and the narrowing is cumulative rather than self-correcting in the absence of deliberate intervention.

The detection threshold

The policy response most commonly proposed to the conditions described above is the development of better AI-content detectors. The framing here matters. Treating the situation as a tool problem invites the expectation that an improved detector will eventually restore verification; treating it as a prevalence problem changes both the diagnostic and the set of plausible interventions.

Detection of synthetic content at scale is constrained by two parameters: the prevalence of synthetic content in the channel being monitored, and the accuracy of the detector being used against that channel. When prevalence rises and detector accuracy does not rise with it, false positives begin to swamp true positives even for detectors that perform well on an absolute basis. At sufficiently high prevalence, a detector that is right most of the time nonetheless produces enough false signals per decision that routine verification becomes operationally infeasible, regardless of which specific detection technique is adopted.

The fraud triangle, first formulated by Donald Cressey, identifies three conditions under which fraudulent behavior becomes likely in a given population: opportunity, pressure, and rationalization. In the specific context of AI-generated content, all three conditions are unusually strong across the general population of writers, rather than being concentrated among a small number of bad actors. Opportunity is high because the cost of producing plausible synthetic prose is near zero and the probability of detection in most institutional settings is modest. Pressure is high because employers, publishers, editors, and instructors continue to reward written output at rates calibrated to an earlier era of production cost. Rationalization is widely available through common framings — “the tool is just a tool,” “everyone is using it,” “the work is still mine” — that have been normalized in public discussion. Under the fraud triangle framework, these conditions predict high rates of undisclosed AI use, and the measured rates reported in the studies cited above are consistent with that prediction.

A second observation concerns the discourse itself. The most widely repeated figure in public discussion of AI-generated content — the claim that 90% of online content will be AI-generated by 2026 — traces through a 2022 Europol report to a line in a 2020 book, which in turn attributed the figure to a single unsourced statement from an executive at a synthetic-media companyX1. The number has been cited in thousands of subsequent sources. It has no denominator, no measurement methodology, and no primary source. It has nonetheless been cited as an authoritative statistic in published scholarly work.

This is a small point about a single number, but it sits inside a larger pattern. Public discussion of AI-generated content has absorbed the same quality-control failures it is attempting to describe. Secondary sources repeat primary estimates without verification. Rounded figures migrate into review articles and then into policy documents. The infrastructure that should be separating signal from noise is itself struggling to do so for numbers about the signal-to-noise problem, which is its own form of evidence about the state of things.

What dies

The title of this essay is less figurative than it may first appear, and less apocalyptic. Written language is not going to disappear. Orders of magnitude more written content is being produced now than in any prior period. The concern addressed here is more specific. It is about the shared assumption, operating beneath most institutional uses of written text, that a given piece of writing reflects a specific person's attempt to communicate a specific argument. That assumption underwrites most of the value we derive from reviewing, citing, grading, and closely reading each other's work. It is also the assumption whose reliability the evidence in this essay calls into question.

The institutions built on top of this assumption — peer review, citation practice, grading, editorial vouching — continue to operate. Reviews are written, papers are published, grades are assigned. The evidence compiled here suggests, however, that the signals those institutions generate about the quality and authorship of submitted work are becoming less reliable as a proxy for the underlying quality they were designed to measure. The rate of that degradation is the open empirical question, and the studies cited throughout this piece represent early efforts to measure it across different institutional settings, with the usual caveats about sampling, detection methodology, and the short time horizon over which any of these measurements have been taken.

The question of what replaces or reinforces the current verification infrastructure is, in my reading, the most consequential one that knowledge-producing institutions will need to address over the next several years. Detection-tool improvements are part of that answer, but they are not the whole answer, because the conditions that produced the current prevalence of synthetic content are upstream of detection. Provenance tracking, institutional disclosure norms, credentialed authorship systems, and shifts in the kinds of work that institutions reward are all plausible components. None of them are close to being implemented at a scale that would match the rate of change documented here.

Summarizing the preceding sections: across student coursework, peer-reviewed publishing, the open web, and the training data pipeline for successor models, the measured share of synthetic content has risen past the point where baseline assumptions of human authorship can be sustained without explicit verification, and the institutional response has not kept pace with that change. My goal in writing this piece has been to compile the evidence for the first claim in a form a researcher can audit, and to make the case that the second claim is worth taking seriously in institutional planning.
